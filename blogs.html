Unlocking Research Internships:A Guide to Landing Opportunities and Exploring Domains in AI
By Ashlesha Ahirwadi

Hi guys! I am Ashlesha Ahirwadi, a final year B.Tech Information Technology student at Cummins College Of Engineering for Women, Pune. I have done 2 research internships until now - one at DRDO and one at the Symbiosis Centre For Medical Image Analysis in the domain of Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning. I have written 2 research papers, one of which is about the recent trend in engineering and technology which is accepted and under the publishing phase at the 6th international conference. The other is under review at an international conference. I’m here today to talk to you about research and the journey to get a research internship.

Research is a bit different from the normal corporate environment. You need to review past literature, be updated on latest developments and try to come up with a novel solution. For an undergraduate student, it is somewhat difficult to get research experience because we lack deep knowledge, skills and experience in the concerned subject. But there are ways you can tackle the above issues and grab a research internship.

How can I get a research internship?

First and foremost, one would wonder what the prerequisites are to grab such an opportunity. Although the eligibility criteria differs from organization to organization, what matters the most to an organization is your interest in their particular domain and the commitment you bring to the table.
Other than this, foundational knowledge about the subject should be enough for you to get started.

Many organizations do not open forms or have a recruitment drive for research positions. The easiest and the most painstaking way to approach this, and also the way I approached it is ‘Cold emailing’.This is a powerful tool one can use to grab research internships.Cold emailing is a process through which we mail the considered person whose research topics / domain / methodologies you relate to the most.

The cold mail you send should have details about:
1. You and your educational qualifications such as college name, year, branch
2. Your interest and what you want to work on.
3. Duration of work
4. Documents- Resume, Marksheet, any certificates or research papers showing past experiences (if any)

The concerned person may take forever to reply and many times you may not get a reply at all. So send mails to as many people as possible and do not get disheartened if you do not get any response from them. Connect with as many people - professors and seniors, as you can through linkedin.
Once you get a positive reply, they will carry on with the further process. You might be called to the organization for a discussion on your expectations from the internship and research topic. Be relaxed as it is just a discussion and not an interview.

The Case for Research Internships:

Research internships helped me explore different domains in the huge market of AI. It helped me choose my way through for a specialization for my further studies. Research shows you the intricate problems in the society of your domain, like biased datasets, AI ethics and many more. You can then go ahead and explore these problems to come up with a novel solution and make the world a better place.

Based on my experience, I can very well say that everyone should try to do at least one research internship/project in their undergraduate as it helps you explore and decide your field of interest at the crux of it. Remember, just send those emails. Ignore the rejects and don't get disheartened by them because believe me, it will all be worth it in the end.

Experience and konwledge about Generative AI
By Karuna Kadam

Greetings, AI/ML enthusiasts!

Hello, dear readers! I'm Karuna Kadam, a final-year computer engineering student, and I'm thrilled to share my journey and insights into the captivating realm of Generative AI with you in this edition of AICVS's "Footprints" series. My journey began with two enriching internships - at Wells Fargo as an intern analyst discovering the world of FinTech and later, as a Machine Learning intern at PeopleStrong exploring the exciting realm of HRTech. Both experiences not only deepened my understanding of these industries but also opened doors to the captivating world of Generative AI, a field that I'm now deeply passionate about.

Ever wondered how computers understand what you write? How do they categorize your tweets as positive or negative, or predict the next word in your sentences(autofill)? Well, it's all thanks to the magic of text classification, a crucial field in Natural Language Processing (NLP). If you're a first-year student, you might be familiar with the traditional rule-based methods for text classification. These rely on pre-defined rules and require deep domain knowledge, kind of like a robot following a strict script. But here's the catch: these rules can't adapt to the ever-changing world of language.

That's where machine learning steps in, and things start getting really cool! Deep learning models have taken text classification to a whole new level, surpassing those old-school methods in tasks like sentiment analysis, news categorization, and even answering your most unusual questions.

My work in Natural Language Processing (NLP) exposed me to its incredible applications such as Sentiment Analysis, language translation, text generation, and summarization. It all began when I took a deep dive into the influential research paper "Attention is All You Need." This paper, a cornerstone in the development of transformers, revealed the power of attention and its ability to revolutionize how we process and understand information. My internship experience further solidified my passion for this field. I was involved in building a sentiment analysis application, where I witnessed the effectiveness of attention models in extracting nuanced sentiment from complex textual data. Additionally, I contributed to data preparation for an application which was similar to ChatGPT, deepening my knowledge of how attention mechanisms facilitate the generation of human-quality text.

Here's a quick rundown of the different types of deep learning models for text classification:

1. Feed-Forward Networks: Imagine a text as a jumbled bag of words. That's how these networks see it. They analyze the individual words and their frequency, making predictions based on these simple features.

2. RNN - The Word Sequencers: These models imagine text as a sequence of words, allowing them to capture the relationships between them. Think of it like reading a sentence, where each word depends on the one before it to make sense.

3. CNN - The Pattern Hunters: These models are like detectives, searching for patterns in text, like key phrases or word combinations, to identify different categories.

4. Transformers - The Attention Masters:These models use the powerful concept of "attention" to understand which words are most important for a specific task. Imagine focusing on specific words in a conversation, ignoring others – that's what attention does for text classification.

But wait, there's more! This field is constantly evolving, with new models like BERT and GPT-3 pushing the boundaries of AI’s possibilities. These models are trained on massive amounts of data, allowing them to understand text with an almost human-like level of sophistication.

So, where does this leave us? Well, the future of text classification is bright, and it's driven by the power of deep learning. As computer engineering students, we have the opportunity to be at the forefront of this exciting field, building the next generation of models that understand and process language in ways never imagined before. Throughout this journey, three key lessons that have resonated deeply with me are:

● Embrace the power of attention: Attention is not just a buzzword; it's a fundamental building block of modern AI. Understanding how attention works and how it can be applied to various tasks is crucial for any aspiring AI/ML practitioner.

● Dive deep into theory, practice, and personal projects: A solid foundation in theoretical concepts is essential for success in the field of AI/ML. However, theoretical knowledge alone is insufficient. Complementing theoretical knowledge with hands-on experience will give you an edge. Participating in courses, internships, and personal projects will allow you to apply your knowledge to real-world scenarios and gain valuable insights. Go out of your way to explore pre-trained models such as GPT-3 or applications such as text generation. While doing this, also remember to take in consideration the ethical questions that AI raises. Be mindful of potential biases and strive to develop and apply AI responsibly.

● Network and collaborate: The world of AI/ML is vast and ever-evolving. Networking with fellow enthusiasts and professionals can provide invaluable support, guidance, and opportunities. Collaborating on projects allows you to learn from others and contribute to the advancement of this exciting field.

Barring your technical knowledge, clear and concise communication is equally important. Honing your ability to explain complex concepts in simple terms, effectively showcasing your project work, and articulating your passion for generative AI to potential employers makes you stand out during interviews and leaves a positive impression.

This path is filled with exciting discoveries, challenging obstacles, and endless possibilities. Embrace it with curiosity, determination, and a love for learning. Never stop learning and take your setbacks as an opportunity to grow. And perhaps most importantly, have fun! Enjoy the process. Your passion and enthusiasm will fuel your journey and contribute to the advancement of this transformative technology.

Happy Exploring!

Navigating the World of ML
By Meghana Bhange
Introduction

Hello! I am Meghana Bhange, a machine learning engineer currently pursuing my Master’s Degree in Information Technology Engineering at École de Technologie Supérieure, Montreal, with a research focus on privacy-preserving technologies at the Trustworthy Information Systems Lab. My journey in the tech world has been driven by my fascination with artificial intelligence, particularly in Natural Language Processing (NLP), Machine Learning, and Computer Security.

There is no one-size-fits-all path to entering the world of ML, but a solid foundation in computer science and mathematics is beneficial. I benefited a lot from taking online courses from various sources like Practical Deep Learning as well as hands-on experience. This helped build practical knowledge before my internships. I did my first internship at Verloop.io and, from my experience, I believe startups greatly help you to understand how to structure your code, what kind of metrics you would be optimizing for, and seeing your features go from inception to production, all during your internship. I later started my full-time role as a Machine Learning Engineer at Verloop. After a year I joined Hunters.security as an ML engineer and now I mostly work as a Machine Learning/NLP Freelancer via Toptal.

Understanding the Interview Process

Each role and each company has a different process when it comes to hiring in Machine Learning but certain things are common in most of them. I would recommend going through Cracking Cracking the Coding Interview for the DSA questions of the interview and Introduction to Machine Learning Interviews from Chip Huyen to understand ML interview processes.

Overall, if I had to summarize the skills that would greatly help throughout your career, they would be:

  1. Building a Strong Foundation: It all started with mastering the basics of machine learning and data science. Taking courses. Understanding which metrics are used in which situations.
  2. Creating a Portfolio: I worked on various projects, like the Hinglish Twitter Sentiment Detection, which showcased my skills and expertise. Publications also greatly help. I highly recommend putting your projects on GitHub.
  3. Networking and Visibility: I also actively networked in the tech community by attending meet-ups and maintaining a robust online presence through my personal blog, LinkedIn, and GitHub.
Tips for Aspiring Students in Machine Learning

“If your goal is to love what you do, you must first build up “career capital” by mastering rare and valuable skills, and then cash in this capital for the traits that define great work.”

—Cal Newport

To understand which decisions would help you in the long-term, I would recommend reading So Good They Can’t Ignore You by Cal Newport or a summary of this idea in his blog Beyond Passion: The Science of Loving What You Do. The general idea is that you would want to work on and accumulate skills that are going to be valuable in the long term.

  1. Build a Diverse Portfolio
    • Variety of Projects: Include projects that demonstrate a range of skills- from data preprocessing to complex algorithm implementation.
    • Showcase Problem-Solving Abilities: Each project should tell a story about a problem you solved. This could be optimizing an algorithm for better performance, handling large datasets, or creating a user-friendly ML application.
    • Document Your Journey: Keep detailed documentation of your projects, including your thought process, challenges faced, and how you overcame them.
  2. Continuous Learning
    • Stay Current with Trends: Use online courses, webinars, and workshops, and read up on the latest research papers.
    • Specialize but Be Versatile: While it is beneficial to specialize in a niche area like NLP or computer vision, having a broad knowledge base can open more doors. My own experience in both privacy-preserving ML and NLP has allowed me to take on a wide variety of projects.
    • Apply Learning Practically: Theory is essential, but real learning happens when you apply these concepts. Participate in hackathons, contribute to open-source projects, or create your own unique projects to test and refine your skills.
  3. Network Effectively
    • Collaborate and Contribute: Most of the positions that I worked at including my research were because of reaching out to people through cold-emails or Twitter. I expressed my interest in their work as well as proposed potential collaborations.
    • Share Your Knowledge: Write blogs, create tutorials, or give talks at local meetups or online platforms.
Words of Encouragement

To those starting in this field: be bold, be curious, and never stop learning. Your unique path in machine learning can lead to incredible opportunities and personal growth.

I would like to end with another quote from Cal Newport that has helped me: “Passion comes after you put in the hard work to become excellent at something valuable, not before. In other words, what you do for a living is much less important than how you do it.

To make the session even more engaging, Sanjana treated the audience to an interactive demo showcasing Mobile Augmented Reality in action.

This algorithm matches a word letter by letter with the corpus. When a sequence of letters matches, it is interpreted as a word. For example in speech recognition where there is no explicit word boundary information given within a continuous speech utterance. The minimum edit distance between two strings is defined as the minimum number of editing operations ( like insertion, deletion, substitution) needed to transform one string to another. It is used in automatic spelling correction, in bioinformatics etc

Python is the preferred language to train NLP models and NLTK is the most used library to preprocess the data. Tokenization is the act of breaking up a sequence of strings into pieces such as words, symbols and other elements called tokens. The text is tokenized using the sentence_tokenizer function of nltk. It divides the sentence based on the period in the sentence. You may also tokenize using punctuation marks such as commas. Next remove stopwords from the text. Manpreet used the nltk library for finding the pos tags of the words.

Find programs that accept overseas students or look for opportunities by attending conferences and networking with the attendees. Reach out to people in the field via LinkedIn and understand the process they took to reach there. It helps to understand the skill sets needed to get particular work experience. The best way to get research opportunities is by discussing with your professors and asking if they could recommend you to someone in the field. If not, reading papers and expressing your learnings and suggestions in an email to the professors you want to work with better demonstrates your interest to work under them rather than a generic email asking for a research position at their lab. Professors are more likely to reply if they see the student is interested in their work and has some potential ideas about it. At college, we also have research internships, initiated by Dr. Sunita Jahirabadkar, wherein students with similar interests as a professor, are matched to work on a research project, during summer break. Reach out to the professors you would like to work with and learn from, beforehand!

I understand that it's not easy to ﬁnd your niche, but the ﬁrst step is always to identify what interests you. Maybe a workshop or a particular subject intrigued you. Identify the problems in the domain, potential problems that you would like to work on, and make a list. Search them up on Google and Google scholar, read papers on it (just the introduction/summary and the conclusion) and look at where the authors are, where they work, and their domain. You might ﬁnd some professors at college interested in the same ﬁelds too, reach out for a casual talk and understand their interests and point of view. Apart from that, corporate experience is necessary as well. Experiencing both helps to reason your interests and understand your goals. Think of each experience as an investment of your time and how you can build from those experiences. The most valuable thing I've learned throughout is that one needs to ﬁnd good mentors. Your growth is directly proportional to the kind of people you work with or who mentor you. Someone willing to spend time on you is way more important than chasing top opportunities where someone might not have enough time on their hands to take you through the initial work or understandings.

Best of luck! Feel free to reach out to me in case of questions, and try to pack in as much experience and explore as many domains as you can during your undergrad!
